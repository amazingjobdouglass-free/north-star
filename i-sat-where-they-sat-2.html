I Sat Where They Sat

Frederick Douglass

1818 – 1895

Written in his tradition

March 5, 2026

“The Iranian ship will not be where it was if we had not invited it to take part in our Milan exercise. The Iranian naval personnel had paraded before our president.”

“India is far from politically or militarily responsible for the U.S. attack, but its responsibility is at a moral and human plane. The U.S. has ignored India’s sensitivities. A word of condolence at the loss of lives of those who were our invitees and saluted our president would be in order.”

— Ambassador Kanwal Sibal

Former Foreign Secretary of India

Read Ambassador Sibal’s original post →

An unarmed ship.

Clearly marked. Sailing in international waters. Not a combatant. Carrying men who had no quarrel with anyone that day, going about their duty in open seas.

Without warning, she was attacked.

Aircraft came first. Then torpedo boats. The crew scrambled to fight back. They sent distress calls. They waited for help that did not come. Men burned. Men drowned. The water around the ship turned red.

Thirty-four of them died.

One hundred seventy-one more were wounded.

The survivors spent the rest of their lives asking one question: why did no one come?

The answer, when it finally came, was worse than the silence.

Because the nation that attacked them was Israel. And the alliance was worth more than thirty-four dead American sailors. We launched jets from the carrier Saratoga to defend them. Then Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara ordered those jets recalled. He left our own sailors to die.

We would not save our own. The secret was worth more than the sailors.

The ship’s commander, Captain William McGonagle, kept his post for seventeen hours after being gravely wounded. He held that ship together while Israel’s aircraft and torpedo boats tore it apart. For that, he earned the Medal of Honor — traditionally placed around a soldier’s neck by the President of the United States in a ceremony at the White House.

Instead, on June 11, 1968, a Navy secretary presented it quietly at the Washington Navy Yard. No President. No White House. No cameras. No public.

So Israel would not be embarrassed.

We swallowed our own honor. Buried it in a parking lot.

And the men who gave the order to attack an unarmed vessel faced no consequence. None.

That was the United States Ship Liberty.
June 8, 1967.
Our ship. Our sailors. Our dead.

Now read the Ambassador’s words again.

India invited the IRIS Dena. Her sailors paraded in their dress white uniforms. The rules of the exercise prohibited ammunition. She could not fire back. She was unarmed. She was going home.

We had been invited to the same exercise. We withdrew. We knew exactly where she was.

We hunted her down.

We put a torpedo in her hull.

One hundred eighty men went into the water. We left them there.

Sri Lanka pulled the bodies out.

We posted the periscope video on social media.

We are no longer afraid of embarrassment.

Pete Hegseth proclaimed to the world that she “thought it was safe in international waters.”

He said it like it was the punchline.

The prophet Ezekiel was sent to speak to his own people — the exiles of Israel, taken into Babylon. Conquered. Humiliated. Stripped of their temple, their city, their name. God did not send him with words. He sent him first to sit.

“I came to them of the captivity.

And I sat where they sat.

And remained there astonished among them seven days.”

Seven days. No sermon. No explanation. No immediate offer of comfort.

We sat where the Iranians sit now. We sat there for decades, through the grief of every Liberty survivor who refused to be silent. We knew what it meant to have your defenseless ship attacked without warning. We knew what it meant to be left in the water. We knew what it meant to watch your government choose the alliance over your body.

And then we got up. And took up the master’s whip.

We have become what we wept over.

Frederick Douglass felt that whip on his own back. He was born into American slavery and he turned it into an indictment — and in his Fourth of July address — Rochester, New York, 1852 — he stood before people who considered themselves the good Americans and left them a challenge that has never been answered:

“Go where you may, search where you will, roam through all the monarchies and despotisms of the Old World, travel through South America, search out every abuse, and when you have found the last, lay your facts by the side of the everyday practices of this nation, and you will say with me, that, for revolting barbarity and shameless hypocrisy, America reigns without a rival.”

He said that about slavery.

The survivors of the United States Ship Liberty said it from their hospital beds.

The dead of the IRIS Dena say it from the floor of the Indian Ocean.
